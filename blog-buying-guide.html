Buying Guide

The Complete Billiard Table Buying Guide for Clubs & Commercial Venues

📅 April 5, 2026 • ✍️ Sundays Editorial • ⏱ 12 min read

Whether you're opening a new billiard club, upgrading a hotel recreation room, or stocking a chain of entertainment venues, the billiard table you choose will define the customer experience for years to come. A wrong choice means warped surfaces, dead cushions, and constant complaints. A right choice means smooth play, minimal maintenance, and customers who keep coming back.

This guide draws from our 19 years of manufacturing experience at Sundays, having supplied 10,000+ tables to 50+ countries. We'll walk you through every factor that matters — and a few that most buyers overlook.

1. Slate: The Heart of Every Billiard Table

The playing surface is the single most important component. There are only two real options for commercial use:

Pro tip: For commercial clubs, 3-piece slate is always better than 1-piece. It's easier to transport, level, and replace if damaged. All Sundays tables use precision-machined 3-piece slate with a flatness tolerance under ±0.1mm.

How to Test Slate Quality

When visiting a factory or inspecting a sample, place a precision level at 5 points on the slate surface (four corners + center). Any reading beyond ±0.1mm indicates insufficient machining. At Sundays, every slate undergoes CNC grinding and manual calibration before assembly.

2. Frame & Leg Construction

The frame holds the slate and absorbs the impact of play. For commercial tables expected to last 10+ years, solid hardwood is non-negotiable:

  • Ash — Excellent shock absorption, straight grain, popular in European-style tables
  • Oak — Dense and durable, resists warping, ideal for humid climates
  • Mahogany — Rich color, premium appearance, standard in luxury installations
  • MDF/Particle Board — Budget option for home use only; not suitable for commercial environments

Leg joints should be bolt-through with cross-brace reinforcement, not just wood glue. This prevents wobble under aggressive play. All Sundays commercial tables use M12 stainless steel bolts with anti-vibration pads.

3. Cushion Rubber: Where Most Cheap Tables Fail

Cushion rubber determines ball rebound accuracy. The industry standard is K-66 profile rubber (the cross-section shape resembles a "K" at 66 degrees). Inferior tables use recycled rubber that hardens within 1–2 years.

Key specifications to demand from your supplier:

  • Material: Natural rubber compound (not synthetic PVC)
  • Rebound consistency: Ball should return to within 5cm of the opposite cushion from a standard test shot
  • Lifespan: Minimum 5 years of daily commercial use before hardening
  • Temperature tolerance: Performs consistently between 15°C and 35°C

4. Cloth: Speed, Durability, and Color

Tournament cloth is 100% worsted wool (no nap). Commercial club cloth is typically a 70/30 or 80/20 wool/nylon blend, offering a balance of speed and durability:

6. Accessories: Don't Overlook the Details

A complete club table setup includes more than just the table:

  • Ball sets — SDAiS Platinum phenolic resin balls offer tournament-grade precision at commercial pricing
  • Cue racks — Wall-mounted or floor-standing, budget 6–12 cue capacity per table
  • Lighting — Professional table lights should provide 520+ lux evenly across the surface with no shadow zones
  • Scoreboard — Electronic or manual, essential for snooker venues
  • Table cover — Fitted dust cover extends cloth life by 30%+

7. Quality Checklist: What to Inspect Before Signing

Before committing to a bulk order, request a sample table or visit the factory. Here's your 10-point inspection checklist:

  1. Slate flatness at 5 points (±0.1mm max)
  2. Cushion rebound test (ball returns consistently)
  3. Frame joint tightness (no wobble when pushed)
  4. Leg height adjustability (leveling feet included?)
  5. Cloth stretch and alignment (no wrinkles, straight lines)
  6. Pocket drop and ball return mechanism
  7. Wood finish quality (no rough spots, even stain)
  8. Hardware quality (bolts, brackets, rails)
  9. Packaging inspection (foam, cardboard, crating)
  10. Certification documents (ISO, SGS, material test reports)
