Factory Sourcing

How to Source Billiard Tables from China: A Distributor's Step-by-Step Playbook

📅 March 28, 2026 • ✍️ Sundays Editorial • ⏱ 15 min read

China manufactures over 70% of the world's billiard tables. Guangdong province alone has hundreds of factories ranging from small workshops to large-scale operations. For distributors and club owners looking to source directly, the opportunity is enormous — but so are the risks if you don't know what to look for.

This guide is based on our experience as a Guangzhou-based manufacturer shipping to 50+ countries. We'll share the insider knowledge that most factories won't tell you.

Step 1: Define Your Specifications Before Contacting Any Factory

The biggest mistake first-time importers make is sending vague inquiries like "I want pool tables, send me your catalog." This signals inexperience and attracts inflated quotes. Instead, prepare a clear specification sheet:

  • Table type: Pool (American), Snooker (English), Chinese 8-ball, or Multi-function
  • Size: 7ft, 8ft, 9ft, 10ft, or 12ft
  • Quantity: Exact numbers, not "maybe 50-100"
  • Target market: US, Europe, Africa, Middle East (affects safety standards)
  • Budget range: Per-unit FOB price expectation
  • Special requirements: Custom colors, logo, packaging

Step 2: Factory Verification — Separate Real from Fake

On platforms like Alibaba, many "factories" are actually trading companies with a markup of 15–30%. Here's how to identify a real manufacturer:

Green Flags (Real Factory)

  • Factory audit certifications: ISO 9001, SGS, SMETA, BSCI
  • Willing to show video calls of the production floor
  • Has in-house engineering team (can modify designs)
  • Production capacity stated in specific numbers (e.g., "800 tables/month")
  • Located in a known manufacturing hub (Guangzhou, Dongguan, Foshan for billiards)

Red Flags (Trading Company or Unreliable)

  • Office address in a residential building or commercial tower
  • Can supply "any product in any industry"
  • No factory photos — only studio product shots
  • Refuses to share production timeline with milestone photos
  • Prices significantly below market (cutting corners on materials)

Step 3: Sample Orders — Non-Negotiable

Never place a bulk order without first receiving a sample. Yes, a billiard table sample is expensive to ship ($300–$600 for international freight), but it's far cheaper than receiving a container of substandard tables.

What to evaluate on the sample:

  1. Slate flatness and surface finish
  2. Cushion rubber bounce consistency
  3. Wood joint quality and finish
  4. Assembly instructions clarity
  5. Packaging integrity after shipping
  6. Does it match the photos and specs you were promised?

Step 5: Quality Inspection Before Shipping

Hire a third-party inspection company (SGS, Bureau Veritas, or TUV) to inspect the finished goods before they leave the factory. A typical inspection costs $200–$400 and covers:

  • Quantity verification
  • Visual defect check (scratches, uneven finish)
  • Dimension verification against specs
  • Packaging integrity test (drop test simulation)
  • Random play-test on assembled sample

A standard 40ft container fits 16–20 pool tables (7–9ft) or 8–10 snooker tables (12ft). Tables are shipped disassembled — legs, frame, and slate packed separately to prevent damage.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. Choosing on price alone — The cheapest quote usually means inferior slate, recycled rubber, or MDF instead of solid wood
  2. Skipping the sample — "Trust me, quality is good" is not a quality guarantee
  3. Ignoring import duties — Billiard tables attract 0–8% duty in most countries; check your local HS code (9504.20)
  4. Not insuring the shipment — Slate cracks happen during transit; always insure for 110% of invoice value
  5. No written contract — Verbal agreements are unenforceable; always have a signed PI (Proforma Invoice) with full specs
